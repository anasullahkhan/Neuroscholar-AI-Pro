N­eu­rovasculoglial cross-talk underlying breakdown of the neurovascular 
unit is a central, yet poorly understood, component of m­an­y n­eu­ro­de­ge­
ne­rative disorders of the CNS, including retinal disease. Primary fatty acid 
amides have been identified to regulate this cross-talk between vasculature 
and neuronal tissues, but specific molecules and mechanisms remain 
unresolved. Here we show, using an unbiased high-resolution metabolomics 
screen, that erucamide, a 22:1 m­o­n­ou­n­s­at­urated omega-9 fatty acid 
amide, is highly dysregulated during photoreceptor degeneration in mice. 
In vivo delivery of erucamide using organosilane-modified porous silicon 
nanoparticles activated retinal myeloid cells, leading to the upregulation of 
angiogenic and neurotrophic cytokines that l­i­m­it­ed v­a­s­cu­lar and neuronal 
degeneration. We identified TMEM19 as a binding protein for erucamide that 
is crucial for myeloid cell activation and subsequent neuroprotection. These 
findings reveal a previously unknown primary fatty acid amide pathway that 
modulates neuroimmune interactions during retinal degenerative diseases. 
We propose erucamide and analogs as candidate therapeutics.
The vast majority of diseases that lead to catastrophic loss of vision do 
so as a result of breakdown in the neurovascular and glial unit of the ret-
ina. Retinal pigmented epithelial (RPE) cell malfunction and subsequent 
photoreceptor degeneration can lead to vision loss, exemplified by con-
ditions such as age-related macular degeneration (AMD)1. Other leading 
causes of vision loss are inherited retinal degenerations such as retini-
tis pigmentosa2,3 and neurovascular retinal diseases such as diabetic 
retinopathy4. These diseases either do not have a clear genetic basis 
(for example, diabetic retinopathy) or are genetically heterogeneous 
(for example, retinitis pigmentosa)5. While these diseases have differ-
ent underlying causes, they share similar pathologies with functional 
loss of the interconnected vasculature, glia, photoreceptors and RPE, 
leading to vascular anomalies and photoreceptor death.
Photoreceptors convert light into electrical signals through a 
collaboration between cells of the neurosensory retina and the RPE 
and choriocapillaris. This phototransduction process begins in the 
photosensitive outer segments of rod and cone photoreceptors.

Erucamide is present in the CNS of many species10,15,19 and has 
been shown to regulate angiogenesis and water balance in skeletal 
muscle and the intestine14,15,19. However, its function in the CNS, biosyn-
thetic pathway, site of action and potential signaling cascades are all 
unknown. In addition, detection of erucamide in human retinal tissue 
has not been previously demonstrated. Using liquid chromatogra-
phy coupled to mass spectrometry (LC–MS), we detected picogram 
quantities of erucamide in peripheral retinal tissue from postmortem 
human donor eyes (n = 6, age range 62–85 years) from Lions NSW Eye 
Bank, Sydney Local Health District Ethics Review Committee (2020/
ETH03339), suggesting that it is ubiquitously expressed in the retina 
across multiple species (Extended Data Fig. 1b,c). As erucamide is 
highly hydrophobic, direct retinal injection is not possible because 
it aggregates locally and fails to disperse within the retina after intra-
vitreal injection. Consequently, we generated functionalized pSiNPs 
for the delivery of erucamide both in vivo and in cultured tissues. 
The pores of these particles were functionalized with hydrophobic 
n-octadecylsilane groups to facilitate loading of erucamide, while the 
overall particles remain sufficiently hydrophilic to allow full dispersion 
in aqueous solutions.
pSiNPs are biocompatible, showing low toxicity in multiple 
endothelial cell types (Extended Data Fig. 2a, average viability exceeded 
85% at all tested concentrations), and can biodegrade into silicic acid 
end products that are readily excreted from the body20. In addition, 
their tunable pore size and their readily functionalized surface make 
them amenable to the loading of a wide range of small-molecule and 
biologic therapeutics. As a result, pSiNPs have been used extensively 
to deliver drugs to sensitive and hard-to-reach tissues21, including the 
delivery of small-molecule drugs to the vitreous22,23. Chemical surface 
functionalization of pSiNPs with n-octadecylsilane (H3Si (CH2)17CH3, 
referred to here as C18) imparts a hydrophobic character24 to the 
mesopores (10–15 nm), and thus were chosen for loading of erucamide 
(Fig. 1g). The nanoparticles maintained desirable sizes (~200 nm) and 
zeta potential25,26 (−33 mV) after erucamide loading, as shown by the 
transmission electron microscopy images, dynamic light scattering and 
zeta potential (ζ-potential) measurements (Extended Data Fig. 2b–d). 
The loading of erucamide was confirmed by the attenuated total reflec-
tance Fourier transform infrared spectrum of the erucamide-loaded 
C18-pSiNPs (Eru-C18-pSi), which displayed the characteristic C = O (at 
1,690 cm−1) and N-H (at 3,310 cm−1) stretching bands of erucamide. 
The C-H scissor and stretching modes (at 1,465 cm−1 and 2,849 cm−1, 
respectively) are assigned to both erucamide and the C18 surface

Distinct lipid mediators, such as specific fatty acid amides, are 
also essential for maintaining retinal health and function. For exam-
ple, ceramides are key players in various forms of photoreceptor 
cell death and are potential drug targets for retinal degenerative dis-
eases8. Anandamide, also known as N-arachidonoylethanolamide, is a 
well-characterized neurotransmitter that regulates the endocannabi-
noid signaling pathway and can modulate visual signal processing by 
interacting with cannabinoid receptors in the retina9. However, little is 
known about the specific roles of primary fatty acid amides (PFAMs) in 
the retina. As a direct extension of the CNS, the retina shares many char-
acteristics with brain tissue. A better understanding of the biological 
and pathological functions of PFAMs in the retina is also likely to shed 
light on their roles in the brain and other neuronal tissues.
PFAMs10,11 represent a family of endogenous chemical messengers 
and signaling molecules in the mammalian nervous system. Struc-
turally, PFAMs consist of a long hydrocarbon chain with an amide 
group (–CONH2) at one end. PFAMs like palmitamide, oleamide and 
the other three PFAMs were first discovered in 1989 in luteal phase 
plasma12. Although their biological functions were initially unclear, 
interest increased when oleamide was identified as an endogenous 
sleep-inducing molecule in mammalian cerebrospinal fluid10. Another 
PFAM, erucamide, was detected in the plasma and cerebrospinal fluid of 
pig brain13 and was first isolated from bovine mesentery. It was reported 
to be pro-angiogenic over 34 years ago14,15, although its complete bio-
logical function remains unclear.
Given the critical role of PFAMs in the nervous system and the 
known importance of other fatty acids in the retina, we undertook an 
untargeted metabolomic and lipidomic screen of normal and degen-
erating retinas. We found that one of the most profound changes in the 
retinal lipid profile occurred with a PFAM, erucamide. Erucamide levels 
decreased drastically in degenerating retinas, indicating a possible neu-
roprotective function. To address the challenge of in vivo delivery of the 
hydrophobic erucamide molecule, we utilized organosilane-modified 
porous silicon nanoparticles (pSiNPs). Intravitreal injections of eru-
camide nanoparticles led to widespread activation of GS-lectin-positive 
cells, which were identified as CD11b+ myeloid cells. This activation, 
driven by TMEM19 protein binding, was accompanied by the upregu-
lation of angiogenic cytokines and growth factors, as well as neuro-
protection, suggesting a new therapeutic function for erucamide in 
retinal diseases.

Erucamide delivery via pSiNPs rescues retinal degeneration
Next, we investigated the potential therapeutic function of erucamide 
in the mammalian retina. rd10 mice were used in these experiments as 
vaso-degeneration and photoreceptor degeneration start at P16–P20 
and occur in a stereotypical temporal sequence29, and erucamide levels 
are spontaneously attenuated by P12 (Extended Data Fig. 1a). We intra-
vitreally injected 214 ng of erucamide into the vitreous of rd10 at P12 
and P22 through Eru-C18-pSi (Fig. 2a). We observed that the extent of 
photoreceptor atrophy was significantly reduced in rd10 mice at P32 
with erucamide. The thicknesses of both the outer nuclear layer (ONL)30 
and the inner nuclear layer (INL) were rescued by erucamide. (Fig. 2b,c). 
To assess retinal function, we performed electroretinography (ERG) 
recordings across a range of stimulus intensities. Erucamide-treated 
rd10 mice exhibited increased scotopic B-wave amplitudes compared 
with vehicle controls across intensities, reaching statistical significance 
at higher flash strengths (50 cd s/m2; Fig. 2d,e). These results indicate 
a rod-dominated, intensity-dependent functional rescue in a geneti-
cally degenerating retina. Additionally, erucamide significantly pro-
tected the intraretinal deep plexus vasculature from vaso-obliteration 
compared to vehicle-injected controls (Fig. 2f,g). Strikingly, we also 
observed significantly increased numbers of CD11b+ myeloid cells 
across the retinal layers, especially in the ONL after erucamide treat-
ment. Our findings are consistent with prior work showing that the neu-
roprotective effect of IGF-1 in rd10 mice also requires CD11b+ microglia, 
which infiltrate the ONL and support photoreceptor survival31. Taken 
together, these data showed that erucamide rescued retinal atrophy 
both morphologically and functionally in rd10 mice.
Moreover, we extended our analysis to the RhoP23H model to test 
whether this pathway applies across different genetic contexts. Unlike 
rd10, which carries a missense mutation in exon 13 of phosphodiester-
ase 6B (Pde6b)29, the RhoP23H model carries the p.Pro23His mutation in 
the rhodopsin gene and shows progressive retinal degeneration similar 
to that seen in patients with autosomal dominant retinitis pigmentosa32. 
As shown in Extended Data Fig. 3, Eru produced clear rescue effects on 
INL thickness and vessel density, and showed intensity-dependent 
functional improvement in ERG responses. In this advanced disease 
stage, vascular density was quantified as a combined all-layer metric 
due to limited anatomical separation of individual plexuses. Together, 
these complementary models support the broad applicability of our 
approach across multiple forms of inherited retinal degeneration.
Erucamide targets CD11b+ myeloid cells
To evaluate the effects of erucamide on healthy ocular tissue and to 
determine the cellular and biological target of erucamide, it was intra-
vitreally administered into 1-month-old wild-type (WT) C57BL/6J mouse 
retinas. Eru-C18-pSi injected mice showed an infiltration of GS-lectin+ 
cells at 3 days after injection, while retinas injected with C18-pSiNPs 
maintained a normal appearance similar to the non-injected control 
group (Fig. 3a). Further staining with collagen IV33 and CD11b34 revealed 
that the GS-lectin+ active signals were CD11b+ myeloid cells but not colla-
gen IV+ endothelial cells (Fig. 3b). These results suggest that erucamide 
might directly target CD11b+ myeloid cells in the retina. We synthesized 
a fluorescent dye (BODIPY)-labeled erucamide (BDPErucamide; Fig. 3c) 
to directly observe the cellular targets. Strikingly, all BDPErucamide was 
taken up by CD11b+ cells, providing additional evidence that CD11b+ cells 
are the direct target of erucamide in the retinas (Fig. 3d). As observed 
in rd10 mice, CD11b+ cells in WT C57BL/6J retinas were activated across 
the retinal vascular layers by erucamide; however, unlike rd10 retinas, 
erucamide-induced CD11b+ cell accumulation in the ONL was not sig-
nificant in WT eyes (Fig. 3e,f). Also, there was no aggressive choroidal 
neovascularization in both rd10 and WT mice after erucamide intra-
vitreal injections (Extended Data Fig. 1d–f).
To further explore the potential effects of erucamide on the target 
cells, we isolated CD11b+ cells by flow cytometry and evaluated the 
expression of pro-angiogenic genes within the cells by quantitative PCR 
(qPCR). We found that the expression of PDGFα, CTGF, PDGFβ, CX3CL1, 
Ang1 and BDNF was higher in CD11b+ cells of erucamide-treated ani-
mals (Fig. 3g). Furthermore, the findings translated to human tissue.

TMEM19 is the binding protein mediating erucamide’s effects
These results raised the question of whether there is a specific recep-
tor or binding protein for erucamide at the cellular level. We used a 
photoaffinity labeling (PAL) method36 to further explore and define 
the potential binding proteins of erucamide in a human microglia cell 
line, HMC3 cells. As shown in Fig. 4, we generated ‘fully functionalized’ 
lipid probes of erucamide (Eru.da) and the closely related PFAMs, which 
are also naturally present in the retina (oleamide and palmitamide, Ole.
da and Pal.da), as control probes36. A photoreactive element (diazirine 
group) was introduced into the carbon chain of erucamide, oleam-
ide and palmitamide to convert reversible small-molecular-protein 
interactions into stable, covalent adducts upon ultraviolet (UV) 
light irradiation. Introduction of a terminal alkyne handle allows 
late-stage conjugation to rhodamine or biotin tags by click chemistry 
(Fig. 4a,b). Erucamide, oleamide and palmitamide probes showed 
overlapping but distinct protein interaction profiles in HMC3 cells 
(Extended Data Fig. 4a). Following PAL (3 h incubation with probes), 
biotin conjugation and affinity enrichment, multiplexed proteomics 
of all three probes provided a unique list of erucamide-specific bind-
ing proteins that are involved in specific biological processes like lipid 
metabolism, signaling and cellular processes. However, a large number 
of these proteins were uncategorized as membrane traffic-related 
proteins (Extended Data Fig. 4b,c). From the list of proteins identified, 
a transmembrane protein, TMEM19, was the most likely candidate to be 
an erucamide binding protein (Fig. 4c) because it was the most highly 
enriched protein in PAL of erucamide diazirine probes compared to 
control probes. We confirmed the interaction between erucamide and 
TMEM19 by overexpressing TMEM19 in HEK293T cells and observed 
that overexpressed TMEM19 is prominently labeled by erucamide 
probes compared to control probes (Fig. 4d). These data indicate that 
TMEM19 is a receptor for erucamide and may mediate erucamide’s 
effect on cell function.

and neurotrophic factors of VEGFa, FGF-2 and TNF.
We found that erucamide is highly abundant in normal retinas and 
is severely dysregulated during retinal degeneration. Erucamide was 
found to be a potent pro-angiogenic, paracrine signaling molecule 
that regulates the retinal angiogenic environment and functions as a 
neurotrophic factor largely by targeting and activating MPCs through 
TMEM19. Knockdown of TMEM19 by siRNA results in failure to respond 
to the erucamide-stimulated paracrine effect of growth factors, which 
is consistent with in vivo knockdown of TMEM19 and inhibition of 
erucamide–TMEM19 downstream pathways by neutralizing antibod-
ies (Fig. 7). These observations provide insight into the action of the 
angiogenic factor erucamide as a cross-tissue signaling molecule. It 
may function similarly in multiple organ systems where it plays a critical 
role in regulating neurovasculoglial cross-talk.
Discussion
In recent years, growing evidence has highlighted that neurovascu-
lar and glial cross-talk is essential for maintaining healthy, functional 
neurons, blood vessels and glia in the CNS40, including the retina. Ther-
apeutic strategies aimed at preventing neuronal degeneration and 
vision loss have focused on modulating neurotrophic and angiogenic 
signaling pathways as well as using iPS cell-derived retinal tissues to 
replace damaged RPE cells/photoreceptors, stabilizing retinal struc-
ture and visual function41–44. Several studies have shown that stem cell 
transplantation into the eyes of retinitis pigmentosa animal models 
can partially improve visual function45,46. However, concerns remain 
regarding the long-term efficacy of iPS cell-derived RPE therapies, 
particularly due to the risk of oncogenic mutations and tumorigen-
esis reported in both preclinical models and clinical observations47,48.

The limited survival rates and potential immune responses pose 
important hurdles to the widespread clinical application of stem cell 
therapies in treating retinal degenerative diseases like AMD and reti-
nitis pigmentosa. Given these limitations, we explored whether small 
molecules expressed by iPS cell-derived retinal tissue could provide 
neurovasculotrophic rescue in models of retinal degeneration. Metabo-
lomic and lipidomic screens of degenerating retinas revealed a PFAM, 
erucamide, with paracrine neurotrophic activity that exerted a pro-
found rescue effect on models of retinal neurovascular degeneration. 
Our approach involved the use of advanced nanoparticles to allow 
effective in vivo delivery of erucamide, circumventing its hydrophobic 
nature and enhancing its bioavailability in the retina.
Furthermore, we identified CD11b+ myeloid cells as primary targets 
of erucamide within the retina. Consistent with prior studies implicat-
ing myeloid cells in photoreceptor support, erucamide treatment pro-
moted CD11b+ cell activation across retinal layers, with significant ONL 
accumulation observed specifically in erucamide-treated degenerating 
rd10 retinas. This context-dependent ONL accumulation of CD11b+ cells 
is consistent with support of neurotrophic signaling to degenerating 
photoreceptors and paracrine angiogenic support of the adjacent deep 
vascular plexus. Further mechanistic studies using PAL, click chemistry 
for biotin-derived affinity enrichment and multiplexed quantitative 
proteomics alongside closely related PFAMs, revealed TMEM19 as a 
new transmembrane protein serving as a major and selective bind-
ing partner for erucamide. Our findings define erucamide–TMEM19 
signaling as a regulator of retinal homeostasis through the coordinated 
modulation of neuronal, glial and vascular compartments. Rather than 
acting solely as a neuroprotective or anti-inflammatory molecule, 
erucamide orchestrates a broader ‘cross-talk’ between neural, vascular 
and glial elements of the retina. Activation of TMEM19 on microglial 
cells stimulates the secretion of cytokines and growth factors (includ-
ing VEGF, PDGF, FGF-2, IGF-1 and BDNF) that support photoreceptor 
integrity and maintain vascular networks.
Although erucamide-treated rd10 mice showed significant B-wave 
recovery under scotopic conditions, A-wave amplitudes exhibited only 
modest, nonsignificant increases, consistent with partial preservation 
of photoreceptors that remain genetically compromised due to the 
Pde6b mutation. This incomplete A-wave recovery suggests that the 
enhanced B-wave likely reflects not only residual photoreceptor input 
but also neurotrophic modulation of inner retinal cells. Supporting 
this, erucamide treatment elevated CD11b+ myeloid infiltration and 
upregulated trophic factors such as FGF, IGF-1 and BDNF, which may 
enhance synaptic function or resilience of bipolar and Müller cells. 
These findings point to a dual mechanism of action: preservation of 
rod photoreceptors and functional support to inner retinal circuits, 
underlying the observed rescue of visual signaling.
TMEM19 is a new transmembrane protein with no reported 
function in any other cell type or tissues. Using single-cell RNA-seq 
databases from the Spectacle Platform, we found that TMEM19 has low 
expression in retinas across different databases for humans and mice, 
with signals mainly from the microglia50, which is consistent with our 
observations. Future investigations will more closely examine the pre-
cise mechanisms regulated by TMEM19 and explore additional applica-
tions of erucamide in both in vitro and in vivo models. Furthermore, the 
optimization of erucamide analogs to enhance solubility and prolong 
in vivo half-life could potentially offer novel small-molecule-based 
therapies for AMD, retinitis pigmentosa and other neurovasculoglial 
degenerative diseases. Conversely, the development of erucamide 
antagonists or TMEM19 inhibitors could provide strategies to mitigate 
pathological angiogenesis associated with conditions like diabetic 
retinopathy and neovascular AMD.
In conclusion, our findings support the use of small-molecule- 
based therapies targeting the erucamide–TMEM19 pathway as a prom-
ising therapy for the treatment of retinal degenerative diseases, offer-
ing potential advantages in terms of efficacy and clinical applicability. 
This discovery expands our understanding of PFAMs beyond their 
established roles in the CNS, shedding light on their potential thera-
peutic applications in retinal degenerative diseases. By elucidating 
the molecular underpinnings of erucamide’s neurotrophic effects, 
this study may advance therapeutic strategies that preserve vision 
and improve quality of life for patients affected by these devastating 
conditions worldwide.

Methods
Animal studies
Animals were used in strict accordance with ethical guidelines of 
the Scripps Research Institute (Scripps Research IACUC protocol 
no. 08-0045). RCS rats were obtained from M. LaVail, UCSF. rd10 
mice (B6. CXB1-Pde6brd10/J), WT mice (C57BL/6J) and RhoP23H mice 
(B6.129S6(Cg)-Rhotm1.1Kpal/J) were obtained from the Jackson Labs. All 
animals were housed under a 12-h light–12-h dark cycle at an ambi-
ent temperature of 22 ± 2 °C and 45–65% relative humidity, with ad 
libitum access to standard rodent chow and water. All intravitreal 
injections were performed under anesthesia to ensure animal welfare 
and procedural precision. Mice were anesthetized via intraperitoneal 
injection of ketamine (100 mg per kg body weight) and xylazine (10 mg 
per kg body weight). For neonatal mice (<P14) with unopened eyes, 
the eyelids were carefully separated using blunt forceps to create a 
small fissure along the natural eyelid margin. To establish an entry 
site, a 0.5-ml 31-gauge insulin syringe (Sol-Millennium, 1653151B-PK) 
was first inserted and withdrawn at the limbus, followed by injection 
using a 5-μl Hamilton syringe fitted with a 33-gauge, 0.5-inch sharp 
needle (point style 4; Hamilton, 7803-15). The needle was advanced 
toward the center of the vitreous cavity while avoiding contact with 
the lens. A total of 0.5 μl of solution was injected into the vitreous cav-
ity for mice younger than P16, and 1 μl for mice older than P16, using 
minimal, constant pressure over 10 s. The needle was maintained in 
position for an additional 10 s to minimize reflux, then withdrawn 
slowly over another 10 s (ref. 52). Success injections were verified by 
the absence of leakage and maintenance of ocular integrity under 
a dissecting microscope. Both male and female mice were included 
in all experimental groups, and no sex-dependent differences were 
observed in their responses to Eru treatment.
For C18-pSi and Eru-C18-pSi injections in rd10, WT and RhoP23H mice, 
bilateral injections were performed. Within the same littermate, pups 
were randomized to control (C18-pSi) or treatment (Eru-C18-pSi) groups.
For BDPErucamide injection, bilateral injections were performed 
for C18-pSi and BDPEru-C18-pSi groups as the original Eru-C18-pSi design.
For in vivo knockdown experiments, all littermate mice received 
intravitreal injections of AAV2 viruses. The left eye was injected with 
an AAV2 carrying a scrambled shRNA control (AAV2-Scr, 2.02 × 1012 
genome copies (GCs) per ml), while the right eye received an AAV2 
expressing shRNA targeting TMEM19 (AAV2-shTMEM19, 2.14 × 1012 
GCs per ml). Five days after the injection, mice were randomly 
assigned to two treatment groups and bilaterally injected with either 
C18-pSi or Eru-C18-pSi nanoparticles. The shRNA sequences used 
were as follows: Scr-shRNA, 5′-CAACAAGATGAAGAGCACC-3′; shT-
MEM19, 5′-ACGTGCAAAAGGTATATAAAG-3′. AAV2 vectors and other 
custom reagents are available from the corresponding author upon 
reasonable request.
For in vivo neutralizing antibody inhibition experiments, the fol-
lowing antibodies were bilaterally co-injected with either C18-pSi or 
Eru-C18-pSi in rd10 mice (vendor, catalog number and concentration 
indicated): TNF (R&D systems, MAB410, 250 ng μl−1), Rat IgG1 Isotype 
Control (control for TNF, R&D systems, MAB005, 250 ng μl−1), FGF-2 
(05-117, Sigma-Aldrich, 1 μg μl−1), Mouse IgG1 Isotype Control (con-
trol for FGF, R&D systems, MAB002, 1 μg μl−1), VEGF164 (R&D systems, 
AF-493-NA, 200 ng μl−1) and Goat IgG Control (control for VEGF164, R&D 
systems, AB-108-C, 200 ng μl−1).
Ocular metabolite extraction and sample storage
Metabolite extractions were performed using either whole eyes (for 
RCS and WT rats) or isolated retinas (for rd10 and WT mice, and human 
donor samples), as specified below. For RCS and WT rat samples, 
rodents were euthanized, and whole eyes were immediately enucle-
ated, rinsed in ice-cold PBS, snap-frozen on dry ice, and stored at 
−80 °C until extraction. For rd10 and WT mouse samples, eyes were 
enucleated and placed in PBS on ice. Under a dissection microscope, 
the retina was carefully separated from the rest of the eyeball and 
immediately processed for extraction. For human donor samples, 
peripheral retina from postmortem eyes was dissected and processed 
similarly. Tissue samples were transferred into 2-ml glass homogeniz-
ers (Wheaton) and homogenized in a cold extraction solvent consist-
ing of methanol–chloroform–water–acetic acid (60:25:14:1, vol/vol), 
a fully miscible mixture optimized for broad-spectrum metabolite 
recovery. The initial homogenization lasted 30 s, followed by the 
addition of 200 μl chilled extraction solvent (4 °C) and further homog-
enization for 90 s. This was followed by two additional homogeniza-
tion cycles using 600 μl and 400 μl extraction solvent, respectively. 
After each cycle, the homogenizer was rinsed thoroughly to minimize 
sample loss. The combined homogenate (~1,200 μl total, including 
eye fluids) was transferred to a pre-chilled microcentrifuge tube and 
snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen for 1 min. All homogenates were kept 
on dry ice during processing. After thawing on ice (4 °C), samples 
were centrifuged at 16,000g for 10–15 min at 4 °C. The supernatant 
was collected and optionally stored overnight at −20 °C to allow fur-
ther precipitation. Samples were then dried using a SpeedVac con-
centrator without heating. The dried extracts were reconstituted in 
acetonitrile–water (1:0.8, vol/vol) containing 5% methanol, 5% chlo-
roform and 0.1% formic acid. Samples were vortexed vigorously or 
sonicated for 1 min, incubated at 4 °C for ~1 h and centrifuged again 
to remove debris. Supernatants were transferred to LC–MS vials for 
metabolomic analysis.
LC–MS
For untargeted metabolites in RCS and WT rats, analyses were per-
formed using a 1260 HPLC system (Agilent Technologies) coupled to 
a 6538 UHD Accurate-Mass Q-TOF (Agilent Technologies) operated

Preparation of pSi
Freestanding pSi flakes were purchased from Trutag, prepared using 
a previously developed ‘perforated etch’ method55. The pSiNPs were 
obtained by immersing the pSi flakes in ethanol in a sealed vial, which 
was then immersed in an ultrasonication bath (Ultrasonic bath, 
97043-960, VWR International) and subjected to ultrasonic fracture 
conditions for 24 h. The nanoparticle suspension was centrifuged 
at 1,500g for 20 min to remove large particles at the bottom of the 
tubes. The nanoparticle suspensions were then centrifuged at high 
speed (10,000g, 20 min) to pelletize the nanoparticles. The superna-
tant was discarded to eliminate the smallest nanoparticle fragments 
that remained in the suspension, and the pellet was resuspended in 
fresh ethanol. The high-speed centrifugation/resuspension proce-
dure was carried out two more times to thoroughly wash the nano-
particles. The resulting pSiNPs displayed an average hydrodynamic 
size of ~170 nm.
Surface modification of pSiNPs
The collected pSiNPs were washed and centrifuged (15,000g, 15 min) 
twice with toluene to remove residual ethanol. The pSiNPs were 
then resuspended in toluene and transferred into a glass vial, and 
n-octadecylsilane was added. The final concentration of pSiNPs and 
n-octadecylsilane was 3 mg ml−1 and 0.6 mg ml−1, respectively. A reac-
tion vessel containing the pSiNPs and n-octadecylsilane was heated 
in a 95 °C oil bath for 24 h with magnetic stirring. The mixture was 
sonicated occasionally during the reaction to ensure full suspension 
of the nanoparticles. The particles were subsequently washed and 
centrifuged three times with toluene and three times with ethanol 
and finally resuspended in 100% ethanol for storage.
Characterization of pSiNPs
Hydrodynamic diameter (size) and the ζ-potential (or surface charge) 
measurements were obtained on a Zetasizer, Zs90 (Malvern Instru-
ments). All pSiNP, C18-pSiNP and Eru-C18-pSiNP size measurements 
and ζ-potential measurements were measured with particles dis-
persed in deionized water. For hydrodynamic size measurements, 
400 μl of 10 µg ml−1 of particles were used while for ζ-potential, 800 μl 
of 10 µg ml−1 of particles were used. The FTIR spectra of the particles 
were obtained with a Thermo Scientific Nicolet 6700 FTIR instru-
ment fitted with a Smart iTR diamond ATR fixture. TEM images were 
obtained using a JEOL 1400 plus electron microscope (JEOL USA) 
operating at 80 kV and subsequently imaged with a Gatan Oneview 
camera (Gatan). Thermogravimetric analysis data were obtained 
using a TA Instruments Discovery SDT 650. Nanoparticle samples 
were heated from 30–950 °C at a ramp rate of 10 °C min−1 in a pure 
oxygen atmosphere. Both weight (%) change and derivative heat 
flow values were assessed, and erucamide content was determined 
by the difference in mass loss between empty and erucamide-loaded 
organosilane-modified pSiNPs. Dye-loaded particle constructs were 
assessed using a Molecular Devices SpectraMax iD5 Multi-Mode 
Microplate Reader. N2 adsorption/desorption isotherms were 
obtained using dry nanoparticles at a temperature of 77 K using a 
Micromeritics ASAP 2020 instrument.
Loading of erucamide to pSiNPs
A 10 mg ml−1 stock solution of erucamide was prepared by dissolving 
erucamide in ethanol overnight. A 3.4 mg ml−1 sample of C18-pSiNPs 
in ethanol was centrifuged (15,000g, 10 min) to remove free ethanol 
and the particles were re-dispersed in 1 ml of the 10 mg ml−1 erucamide 
stock solution. The particles were then subsequently allowed to mix at 
20 °C overnight. The particles were then centrifuged (15,000g, 10 min) 
and washed three times with PBS to remove any unbound erucamide 
and were resuspended in PBS or media and were used immediately 
after resuspension.

PAL method
HMC3 cells and HEK293T cells were treated with vehicle (ethanol) or a 
lipid probe for 3 h in the incubator. HMC3 cells were then UV irradiated

to biotin azide by CuAAC reaction. The biotin-labeled proteins were 
then enriched with streptavidin beads and trypsin digested, and the 
digested peptides were labeled with TMT, followed by combination, 
HPLC fractionation and LC–MS/MS analysis36. HEK293T cells were then 
UV irradiated then UV irradiated and lysed, and lipid probe-labeled pro-
teins were conjugated to Rhodamine by CuAAC reaction. The labeled 
proteins were then used for visualizing by western blot.

HMC3 cells and HEK293T cells were treated with vehicle (ethanol) or a

RNA isolation, real-time PCR and RNA-seq
For whole retina and culture cells, single retinas were collected in 
500 μl of TRIzol and total RNA was isolated using a PureLink RNA Mini 
Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s instruc-
tions. Seven hundred and fifty nanograms of RNA was used for RT–qPCR

Scientific). For flow-sorted cells, total RNA was isolated from sorted 
cells using the RNeasy Micro Kit (QIAGEN) and reverse transcribed 
using Maxima First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit for RT–qPCR (Thermo 
Scientific). qPCR was performed using Power-up SYBR Green PCR 
Master Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and primers on a Quantstudio 
5 Real-Time PCR System (Thermo Fisher Scientific). β-actin (Actb) was 
used as the reference gene for all experiments. Levels of mRNA expres-
sion were normalized to those in controls as determined using the 
comparative CT (ΔΔCT) method. RNA samples were used for RNA-seq 
in Scripps Genetic Cores. Quality control of raw sequencing data was 
performed using FastQC and MultiQC56. Raw reads were aligned 
to the reference genome using RSubread57. Gene expression levels 
were quantified using featureCounts. Post-alignment quality control 
included assessing the distribution of reads across genomic features 
(for example, exons, introns) and checking for potential batch effects 
using principal component analysis. Differential expression analysis 
was carried out using the limma-voom pipeline1. Data were filtered and 
normalization was performed using the trimmed mean of M-values 
method. Linear modeling and empirical Bayes moderation were applied 
to identify differentially expressed genes. Genes with a false discovery 
rate-adjusted P value < 0.05 were considered significantly differen-
tially expressed. Overrepresentation analysis and functional enrich-
ment analysis were performed with the clusterProfiler58 package using 
Gene Ontology and KEGG to define biologically relevant pathways and 
enrichment analysis was conducted.
Cryosection and immunohistochemistry
For cryosections, eyecups were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) at 
4 °C for 4 h. Under a dissecting microscope, a cut was carefully made 
around the cornea, and the eyecups were transferred to 20% sucrose in 
PBS at 4 °C for overnight. Samples were embedded in OCT compound 
(Tissue-Tek, 4566) with the eyecup oriented dorsal side up and the optic 
nerve positioned at the bottom of the cryomold, then snap-frozen in 
chilled isopentane on dry ice. Once frozen solid, blocks were wrapped 
and stored at –80 °C until sectioning. For cryosections, blocks were 
equilibrated in the cryostat chamber (–20 °C) for 1 h, mounted dorsal side 
down (optic nerve at the bottom), and sectioned at a thickness of 10 µm. 
Sections were air-dried and processed for immunohistochemistry using 
standard protocols59. Briefly, slides were permeabilized and blocked in 
2% BSA in PBS containing 0.1% Triton X-100 at room temperature for 1 h, 
followed by incubation with primary antibodies at 4 °C overnight. The 
following primary antibodies were used: Recoverin (rabbit; Millipore, 
AB5585; 1:500 dilution), isolectin GS-IB4 (Griffonia simplicifolia, Alexa 
Fluor 568 conjugated; Thermo Fisher Scientific, I21412; 1:1,000 dilution). 
Secondary antibodies were obtained from Life Technologies, and the 
DAB staining kit was purchased from Vector Laboratories.
Retinal whole-mount staining
Retinal whole mounts were prepared as previously described with 
minor modifications52. Eyecups were fixed in 4% PFA at 4 °C for 1 h, then 
transferred to individual wells of a 48-well plate containing PBS. The 
cornea was carefully removed using fine forceps and scissors, followed 
by gentle separation of the sclera and choroid from the retina, avoiding 
direct contact with the retinal tissue to prevent damage. The lens and 
hyaloid vasculature were removed, and the isolated retina was used 
for immunohistochemistry. Samples were incubated with primary 
antibodies overnight at 4 °C. Antibodies of collagen IV (Millipore, 
AB756P; 1:200 dilution) and CD11b (BD Biosciences, 550285; 1:500 
dilution) were used. The following day, tissues were washed and incu-
bated with secondary antibodies for 2 h and then DAPI staining at room 
temperature. Following final PBS washes, each retina was transferred 
to a six-well Teflon-coated slide with 8-mm diameter wells (Electron 
Microscopy Sciences, 63423-08), positioning the optic nerve head at 
the center. Four to six evenly spaced radial incisions were made from 
the periphery toward the center to flatten the retina into a ‘flower-like’ 
configuration. Retinal petals were gently unfolded and flattened with 
the vitreous surface facing upward. Excess buffer was removed, and a 
drop of SlowFade mounting medium (Molecular Probes) was added 
before applying a coverslip. Slides were imaged immediately or stored 
at 4 °C in the dark until confocal microscopy.
Choroid flat-mount staining
Eyecups were fixed in 4% PFA at 4 °C for 1 h and transferred to individual 
wells of a 48-well plate containing PBS. The cornea was removed with 
fine forceps and scissors, followed by careful separation of the RPE–
choroid–sclera complex from the rest of the eyeball. To remove RPE 
cells, the remaining tissue was incubated in freshly prepared 1% EDTA 
in PBS for 2 h at room temperature. For melanin bleaching, tissues 
were incubated in 10% hydrogen peroxide (Sigma, H1009) diluted in 
PBS at 55 °C for 90 min in a water bath. After bleaching, tissues were 
washed three times in PBS and twice in PBS containing 0.1% Triton 
X-100. Immunohistochemistry staining was then performed using the 
same procedure as described for retinal whole mounts.
Confocal microscopy and quantification
All images were acquired using a confocal laser-scanning microscope 
(LSM710, Carl Zeiss) and processed with ZEN 2010 software (Carl Zeiss) 
and ImageJ/Fiji (National Institutes of Health or NIH). For retinal thick-
ness analysis, recoverin- and DAPI-labeled cryosections were imaged 
at high resolution. Thickness was measured at eight equidistant radial 
points extending from the optic nerve toward the peripheral retina 
on both the nasal and temporal sides. Mean values were calculated 
for each eye. Eyes injected with erucamide or vehicle (n ≥ 5 per group) 
were compared. The full procedure was repeated in four independent 
experiments. Statistical analysis was performed using two-way ANOVA, 
and data were plotted with GraphPad Prism 10. For vascular density 
analysis and CD11b+ cell quantification, z-stacks of three-channel con-
focal images (collagen IV, CD11b and DAPI) were acquired at 5-μm step 
intervals across the full retinal thickness to capture the superficial, 
intermediate and deep plexus layers using retinal whole-mount prepa-
rations. Maximum intensity projections were generated using ImageJ/
Fiji. Images were split into individual grayscale channels and thresh-
olded to isolate vascular structures. Vascular density was calculated 
as the percentage of vessel-covered area relative to the total selected 
retinal area using the ‘Threshold’ and ‘Vascular density’ plugins in 
ImageJ/Fiji. CD11b+ cells were quantified from merged three-channel 
images using the ‘Cell Counter’ plugin in ImageJ/Fiji. Cell counts were 
performed independently in each vascular layer as well as the ONL.

Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 10 software. 
Unless otherwise specified, data are presented as the mean ± standard 
deviation or standard error of the mean. Comparisons between two 
groups were made using two-tailed unpaired t-tests. For two-factor 
experimental designs, two-way ANOVA followed by Fisher’s LSD post 
hoc test was applied. For multiple-group comparisons, the two-stage, 
step-up method (Benjamini, Krieger and Yekutieli) was used for control-
ling the false discovery rate in multiple unpaired t-tests. A P value < 0.05 
was considered statistically significant. All data points represent indi-
vidual biological replicates, and no statistical methods were used to 
predetermine sample size. All experiments were repeated indepen-
dently at least three times.
Statistics and reproducibility
No statistical method was used to predetermine sample size. No data 
were excluded from the analyses. For in vivo experiments using rd10, WT 
C57BL/6J and RhoP23H mice, animals within the same litter were ran-
domly assigned to treatment groups (C18-pSi vehicle or Eru-C18-pSi). 
For the AAV2 knockdown experiments, each mouse received contralat-
eral eye injections (AAV2-Scr in the left eye, AAV2-shTMEM19 in the right 
eye), with subsequent bilateral assignment to vehicle or erucamide 
treatment randomized within litters. Investigators were not blinded 
to group allocation during in vivo experiments; however, quantifica-
tion of retinal layer thickness, vascular density, CD11b+ cell counts 
and ERG analysis were performed using standardized automated or 
semiautomated methods to minimize subjective bias. For in vitro 
experiments (iPS cell-MPC treatments, PAL assays, qPCR), treatments 
were performed in parallel under identical conditions. All experiments 
were independently repeated at least three times with similar results, 
except where otherwise stated in the figure legends.

Methodology
Sample preparation
A postnatal neural dissociation kit (Miltenyi, 130-092-628) was used to prepare a single cell suspension from mouse retinas. 
Cells were centrifuged at 150 xg for 5 min at 4°C. The digested tissue was resuspended in 100 μL of 4% FBS in PBS containing 
an FITC antibody to CD11b (1:100; BioLegend, 101206) and incubated for 20 min on ice. The cells were washed and 
suspended with 1 ml of 4% FBS/PBS containing DAPI (1:2000; Thermo Fisher Scientific, 62248). 
Instrument
Beckman Coulter, MoFlo Astrios EQ cell sorter equipped with 405nm, 488 nm, 561nm and 640nm excitation lasers. 
Software
Beckman Coulter, Summit Software, version 6.3.1. 
Cell population abundance
3000-7000 cells/sample

Step 1: Sort Plots
