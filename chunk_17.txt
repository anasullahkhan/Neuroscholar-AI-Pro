Enrich- ment analyses on these results revealed that the most significantly decreased pathway involves cytokine–cytokine receptor interactions (Fig. 5b and Extended Data Fig. 5a–c). In fact, 42 genes in this pathway were activated by erucamide stimulation in scrambled cells but dys- regulated in siTMEM19 cells. A number of them (for example, PDGFRB, TNFSF18, TNFSF13B and TNFRSF8) are reported to be highly associ- ated with angiogenesis and vascular homeostasis37–39 (Fig. 5c). qPCR confirmed that the effective downstream cytokines/growth factors (VEGFa, PDGFα, FGF, TNF, IGF-1 and BDNF) of these pathways are upreg- ulated by erucamide, but they were dysregulated when we knocked down TMEM19 (Fig. 5d). This aligns well with our results indicating an upregulation of pro-angiogenic genes in erucamide-injected reti- nas. To determine if TMEM19 is essential for the activation of MPCs by erucamide stimulation, we knocked down another two top candidates from the same PAL list. It was only when TMEM19 was knocked down that we observed that most of the growth factors (for example, FGF-2, VEGFa and PDGFα) triggered by erucamide were not upregulated or activated (Extended Data Fig. 5d,e). Interestingly, the general activa- tion of MPCs by TGFβ1, lipopolysaccharides or dimethyloxalylglycine was not affected by knocking down TMEM19 (Extended Data Fig. 5f). These results suggest that TMEM19 is an authentic binding protein for erucamide and is essential for pro-angiogenic and neurotrophic signaling pathways mediated by erucamide in MPCs.
